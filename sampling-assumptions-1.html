7 Sampling assumptions

So far, we’ve seen where strong sampling was a logical assumption to make. But it isn’t always.

For example, consider the hormone problem from Chapter 4. If you were randomly testing people’s hormone levels and every person just happened to be healthy, clearly strong sampling wouldn’t be appropriate, because we were sampling from the full population of both healthy and unhealthy people.

This raises a question: Are people sensitive to the process by which data are sampled?

Spoiler alert: Yes.

7.1 Word learning

Suppose you see the following collection of objects on a table.

Figure 7.1: From Xu & Tenenbaum (2007).

Now consider the following situation:

A teacher picks three silver peace signs from the pile and calls them “wugs.”

What is a wug? Are all peace signs wugs? Or just silver peace signs?

Consider a different situation:

A teacher picks one silver peace sign from the pile and calls it a “wug.” The teacher then asks a child to choose two more (the child picks two more silver peace signs). The teacher confirms that they are also wugs.

What is a wug? Are all peace signs wugs? Or just silver peace signs?

In both situations, the observed data is the same: three silver peace signs labeled as wugs. What’s different is the process by which the data was collected. In one case, a knowledgeable teacher picked all the wugs; in the other, the teacher only picked one example and (probably not knowledgeable) child picked the others.

To use some terms we’ve seen before, the teacher is using strong sampling. The child is using something closer to weak sampling.

Figure 7.2: The hierarchy of objects. From Xu & Tenenbaum (2007).

This is based on an actual study done by Fei Xu and Josh Tenenbaum, who also developed a model of the task. They asked whether children and adults would generalize the word “wug” to the basic-level category (peace signs) or the subordinate category (silver peace signs). Here are their results:

Figure 7.3: Experimental results from Xu & Tenenbaum (2007).

As you can see, people clearly distinguished between the teacher-driven situation and the child-driven (learner-driven) situation, and were much more likely to generalize “wugs” to the subordinate category when the teacher picked the objects.
